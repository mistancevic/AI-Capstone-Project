# Evidence — Investigation 5: Coach-Side (Expert / Key-Informant, Part A desk) + Interview Guide (Part B)

Status: **completed — and it is the weakest, most disconfirming pass in the
program.** This matters more than its length: the coach is the buyer, the
felt-pain holder, and the distribution channel in our entire strategy, and
the desk evidence for that does **not** yet hold up. Sources are ~70% vendor
marketing (companies selling coaching software assert exactly our
hypothesised pain — marked weak regardless of fit). The run could not reach
practitioner forums (r/personaltraining, r/nutrition) — a material gap.
Sources not yet spot-checked.

This is a **supply-side / business-thesis** channel, not a journey-map
sentiment channel — so it does **not** change the valley triangulation from
investigations 1–3. What it hits is the coach-channel bet in
`strategy-notes.md`. Read accordingly.

## The blunt summary

The desk pass could **not** find, from the coach's own mouth:
- coach *attribution* of roster churn to "life happening" (only client-side
  self-report exists);
- how much time coaches lose to routine "what do I eat instead?" questions
  (**no credible source at all**);
- willingness to pay for an adherence tool (**no signal**);
- any support for the skip-as-default mechanism (**zero**).

And it found two things that cut *against* the concept (below). Net: the
coach-side felt-pain claim is **unvalidated by desk research** and moves to
primary research — the Part B interviews and the founder's own coaching
network. It is not disproven; it is unevidenced.

## What the desk pass did support (weakly)

- **Weekly check-in is the dominant cadence**; adherence is read from weight
  trends, photos, self-rating — not continuous data. (coach-education
  material, moderate)
- **Adherence, not plan design, is the usual failure point** — coaches are
  taught to check consistency before adjusting macros. ("Most of the time,
  the plan isn't the issue; your client's consistency is." —
  nutritioncoachingacademy.com, May 2025, moderate)
- **Self-report and log diverge** — clients believe they followed the plan
  but skipped logging. (vendor, weak)
- **The disappearing-client cycle is real in certification material** —
  Precision Nutrition: "Client shows up, you work hard on them, they
  disappear." (moderate)

## The single strongest number — but it is client-side, not coach-side

**37% of 400 program dropouts cited competing priorities / lack of time as
their exit reason — the most-cited reason** (Zizzi et al., *Journal of
Obesity*, 2016). Corroborated by smaller qualitative work: telenutrition
dropouts 50% work/university pressure, 60% marital responsibilities (n=10,
2024); NHS low-calorie-diet dropouts produced "life gets in the way" as a
core theme (n=10, 2024); a CBT-for-obesity review lists job difficulties and
logistics among dropout causes (≥50% of studies high-bias).

This is the best evidence in the whole program that **"life happening" ends
plans.** But it is *dropouts self-reporting to researchers*, not *coaches
attributing churn*. Assuming the two match is the largest inferential leap
in the report — and it is exactly the leap our coach-channel thesis makes.

## Disconfirming evidence — the part that changes the strategy

1. **Coaches are actively advised *against* mid-week monitoring.** A 2026
   coaching guide argues checking in mid-week is a *mistake*: it trains
   clients that the weekly submission doesn't matter, doubles admin, and
   creates dependency (usecoached.com, Jan 2026). A real-time
   disruption-flagging tool meets a **considered ideological objection**
   from a segment of the buyer base — a philosophy conflict, not a feature
   gap.
2. **Some coaches treat disruption as a *filter*, not a problem to solve.**
   One trainer terminates clients who miss check-ins ("If you miss
   check-ins, I will terminate your plan"), framing it as not wasting the
   client's money. Selling "help clients survive a broken day" assumes the
   coach *wants* to retain those clients. Some don't.
3. **Detection-lag evidence is circular** — every source claiming coaches
   find out too late is a company selling the fix. Evidence a funded
   category exists; not evidence the pain is real and unmet.
4. **Tooling complaints are about reliability, not blind spots** — coaches
   complain about crashes, sync, speed (Trainerize: 75% negative across 64
   crash mentions), not "I can't tell when a client is struggling." A
   different product than ours.
5. **Plateau confounder** — dropout literature repeatedly cites
   dissatisfaction with *results* and unrealistic expectations, so apparent
   "disruption churn" may be outcome disappointment wearing a scheduling
   excuse.

## What this forces on the strategy (see strategy-notes.md)

- The **coach-as-felt-pain-buyer** claim (3rd argument for the coach
  channel) is **unvalidated by desk research** — demote from "established"
  to "hypothesis for primary research." The client-side 37% is suggestive,
  not a substitute.
- **The philosophy conflict is designable, and the escalation-agreement is
  the answer.** PlateMate already lets the coach configure what gets
  flagged. A coach who ideologically rejects mid-week visibility sets
  escalation to *none* and uses PlateMate purely as client-side adaptation
  (the client still gets the next-bite answer; the coach isn't pulled in).
  The "I want to know" coach and the "don't make me babysit" coach are
  served by the same configurable escalation — this is a selling point, not
  a contradiction. The **filtering coach** (terminates on missed check-ins)
  is simply not our customer; do not design for them.
- **Willingness to pay and coach time-cost are now primary-research jobs** —
  Part B Q4/Q8 and survey Q5. No desk proxy exists.

## Part B — Key-informant interview guide (now essential, not optional)

Target: 3–5 practicing nutrition/physique coaches with ≥5 active paying
clients. ~10 min. Read-aloud framing: *"I'm researching how coaching
practices operate day to day. No right answers, I'm not selling anything.
I'd like your actual last two weeks, not your ideal process."*

Discipline: after every answer, ask "walk me through the last time that
happened." Anecdotes are data; generalisations are opinion. **Do not react
positively to hypothesis-confirming answers** — coaches read enthusiasm and
feed you more of it.

1. Walk me through building a plan for your most recent client. What did you
   decide first, and what took longest?
2. Between the last two check-ins with one client, what did you actually see
   or hear from them? Chronologically.
3. Think of the last client whose week went off-plan. When did you learn
   about it, and how?
4. When a client contacts you between check-ins, what about? What did the
   last two or three messages actually say?
5. Of things happening in your clients' weeks now, which would you want to
   know about within the day, and which would you rather not hear about at
   all? What makes the difference?
6. Some coaching tools send clients automatic messages or suggestions.
   Your experience with that, if any — and where would you draw a line?
7. How do you talk to a client about a day that didn't go to plan? Words or
   framings you deliberately avoid, and why?
8. Walk me through the last tool or subscription you paid for in your
   practice. What made you pay, what did it replace, what would make you
   cancel?

Optional close (only after Q8, only if time): *"Last thing, skip if you
like. I'm exploring a concept for helping people stay on a nutrition plan
when their day falls apart. If it existed, what's the first objection you'd
raise?"*

Wording rationale: Q5/Q6 are two-sided so "nothing" / "I'd hate that" comes
back frictionlessly (the disconfirming evidence says some coaches will).
Q7 avoids naming "compensation"/"restriction" so you learn whether tone is
even on their radar unprompted. Q8 asks revealed spending, not hypothetical
WTP (near-worthless in interviews).
