## Hard numbers worth citing

- Diet-app abandonment: **86%**; median health-app abandonment ~70% at 100 days (Kidman 2024, 18 studies, n=525,824).
- **2.58%** of 189,770 downloaders became active users of a food-photo journal (Helander 2014; dated).
- **<50%** still tracking at week 10 under active RCT support (Harvey 2019).
- Dietary adherence decay: **81% (month 1) → 57% (24 months)** (Hall & Kahan 2021).
- **51%** of US employees skip lunch ≥1×/week; 33% ≥2×/week (ezCater 2025, n=1,000; vendor-sponsored — caveat).
- Strict-diet app users active at **14.31%** vs 3.99% for no-defined-diet users (χ² p<.001) — rigidity correlates with retention.

## What the evidence changes (design & positioning implications)

1. **The disruption valley has two failure modes, one intervention.** Skip
   (time-scarcity path; our persona's observed behavior) and binge (violation
   path; the literature's modal behavior — "the diet is already broken, so
   eat and enjoy"). PlateMate's intervention answers both: the next
   plan-consistent action, fast, with the "day still works" math. The
   what-the-hell effect is triggered by the belief that the day is ruined —
   the multi-day averaging line, pivot-as-success framing, and banned
   compensation language are precisely a counter to that belief. The product
   was designed against the real mechanism; now it can be argued with
   citations.
2. **Outcome wording (already adopted) is now evidence-backed:** "the client
   takes the next plan-consistent action instead of skipping, compensating,
   bingeing, or abandoning the day" — not "the day lands on plan."
3. **Drop "decision paralysis" as causal language** everywhere — ego
   depletion failed a 23-lab replication (d=0.04). Keep the observed
   behavior: with no clear compliant option and no time, the meal gets
   skipped (direct coaching observation). Describe behavior, not the
   contested construct.
4. **Logging fatigue may be the deepest valley** — reorder in journey map v2
   (eat-&-log deeper than or equal to disruptions). The wedge does not
   change: depth of pain ≠ white space, and logging is the
   competition-saturated valley. But the one-tap-confirm side effect gets
   promoted in the story.
5. **Rigidity is a feature** — strict plans remove planning burden and
   correlate with retention; flexible-vs-rigid RCT found no adherence
   difference, with rigid possibly easier to follow. Validates
   coach-plan-authoritative design. Positioning line: *structured plans work
   because they remove decisions; they fail at disruptions; PlateMate adds
   flexibility only where the structure breaks.*
6. **Split "plateaus & boredom"** in map v2: plateau (physiological →
   coach expectation-setting, a coaching-layer response) vs boredom
   (motivational → variety features help abandonment risk, not outcomes).
7. **Timing caveat:** EMA data locates lapses at home/evenings/weekends —
   the evening/social triggers in our design matter at least as much as the
   noon-meeting scenario; demo scenarios should keep the colleague-dinner
   case prominent.

## The decisive open question (primary research)

**When the plan breaks on a workday, does the persona skip or overeat?**
Directly tested by peer-survey Q3 (open text, "what do you do when it
happens?") and to be added to the coach interview guide (investigation 5,
part B). Everything about the -25 valley's story depends on the split.

## Gaps the literature cannot fill

No per-journey-stage sentiment data exists for any nutrition product; no
adherence data for software professionals as a segment; no time-to-log
measurements. The map's -100..+100 depths remain our hypotheses — the
ordering is now partially evidence-constrained, the magnitudes are not.
